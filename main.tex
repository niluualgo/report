%---------------- ABSTRACT ----------------%
\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

In high-density urban environments, losing a personal item is often a chaotic experience defined by panic and a severe lack of actionable data. Current methods for retrieving lost items rely heavily on traditional bulletin boards, unstructured social media posts, or unverified chat groups. This reliance creates an "illusion of recovery," where people falsely believe lost items are easily found, despite statistics showing extremely low recovery rates and high instances of fraud.

To address these critical flaws, this project proposes "Reunite" , a comprehensive mobile application that transforms the lost-and-found process from a passive waiting game into an active, algorithmically secured recovery mission. The core innovation of the system lies in the establishment of a "Trust Network." The system prevents the prevalent "Claim Scam" through an Asymmetric Privacy protocol, which obfuscates the visual details of found items until the claiming party can prove ownership. Furthermore, the application ensures secure physical handovers utilizing a cryptographic QR-code verification process. 

The application is powered by a custom matching and scoring engine that calculates the probability of a match based on geospatial proximity, time freshness, and attribute similarity. To handle the visual map clutter common in high-density areas, the frontend employs a Greedy Clustering algorithm ($O(N)$) on top of Mapbox SDK. Built using Flutter for cross-platform compatibility and Supabase for millisecond real-time data synchronization, Reunite aims to eliminate "ghost data" and provide a highly efficient, scalable, and secure recovery platform for modern communities.

%---------------- CHAPTER 1 ----------------%
\chapter{Introduction}

\section{General Background}

The experience of losing a valuable personal item in a high-density urban environment—such as a university campus, a bustling city center, or a public transit hub—is universally stressful. This experience is characterized by immediate panic and a complete lack of actionable data. The individual rarely knows exactly when or where the item was dropped, and the traditional infrastructure for recovering such items is severely outdated. 

Society currently suffers from an "illusion of recovery." People convince themselves that if an item is lost, a good Samaritan will simply hand it over to a front desk or post about it online, and the item will easily find its way back. However, evidence and recovery statistics heavily contradict this. In reality, we rely on fragmented, unstructured methods to find these items. People post on local WhatsApp groups, Instagram stories, or unregulated online forums. Because these platforms were not designed for secure item recovery, the data is unorganized, unsecured, and highly susceptible to malicious actors.

As urban density increases and the average individual carries more high-value portable electronics (smartphones, earbuds, laptops), the volume of lost items has grown exponentially. The difficulty in securely reuniting these items with their rightful owners requires a dedicated, technologically advanced solution that prioritizes data organization, spatial awareness, and user security.

\section{Problem Definition and Identification}

The current manual and digital methods of managing lost and found items suffer from several critical, systemic flaws that hinder successful recovery and endanger users:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{The Claim Scam:} When individuals find an item and post it online, they typically upload a clear, high-resolution photo of the object. Malicious actors use these publicly posted photos and details to fake ownership, claiming the item is theirs and stealing valuables.
    \item \textbf{Spatial Blindness:} Traditional lost-and-found posts rely on vague textual descriptions (e.g., "I lost my keys near the big tree by the library"). These descriptions leave searchers spatially blind, unable to accurately pinpoint recovery zones or track the trajectory of where the item might have been moved.
    \item \textbf{Map Clutter:} Existing digital solutions that attempt to use map interfaces often fail to scale. In busy areas, hundreds of lost and found pins overlap on the map, creating severe visual clutter that renders the digital maps entirely unusable.
    \item \textbf{Ghost Data:} Old, unresolved posts stay up on social media forums and legacy apps forever. Because there is no real-time synchronization or automated cleanup, the system becomes clogged with outdated, irrelevant information, making it impossible to find fresh data.
\end{itemize}

\section{Objective}

The primary objective of Reunite is to transition the lost-and-found process from a passive "waiting game" into an active, algorithmically secured recovery mission. 

Specifically, the objectives are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item To build a secure "Trust Network" that protects the identity and property of users.
    \item To automate the matching process between lost and found items using a robust, multi-variable scoring engine.
    \item To provide clear, un-cluttered geospatial visualization of lost items using advanced clustering algorithms.
    \item To guarantee the security of physical item handovers by implementing a cryptographic QR-code verification protocol.
\end{itemize}

\section{Scope}

The scope of the project encompasses the end-to-end development of a cross-platform mobile application named "Reunite." The frontend is developed utilizing the Flutter framework to ensure seamless operation on both Android and iOS devices. The backend relies on Supabase to handle relational data (PostgreSQL) and push real-time updates via WebSockets. Key integrations include the Mapbox SDK for complex geospatial clustering and the development of custom matching algorithms on the server-side to automate discovery. The project focuses strictly on community-driven recovery and does not involve GPS hardware tracking tags (like Apple AirTags), but rather crowdsourced spatial data.

%---------------- CHAPTER 3 ----------------%
\chapter{System Requirement}

\section{Introduction}

System requirements define the absolute necessary resources and environmental conditions required for the successful development, deployment, and operation of the Reunite mobile application. Because the application relies heavily on dynamic geospatial mapping, camera integration, and real-time database synchronization via WebSockets, adequate network and hardware capabilities on the user's mobile device are critical. 

\section{Hardware Requirements}

To ensure that the application runs smoothly and provides accurate spatial data, the following hardware specifications are required:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Device Types:} Modern Android or iOS Smartphones. The application is not optimized for non-touch desktop environments.
\item \textbf{Camera Module:} A functional rear-facing camera is strictly required. This is utilized for capturing high-resolution photos of lost/found items and for scanning QR codes during the secure handover phase.
\item \textbf{GPS / Location Services:} Active GPS hardware is required for accurate geolocation features. This allows the app to render the user's location on the map and is the foundational metric for calculating the "Proximity Score" ($S_{geo}$) in the matching engine.
\item \textbf{Network Connectivity:} A stable internet connection (4G, 5G, or broadband Wi-Fi) is required to maintain the WebSocket connection for real-time updates and to fetch map tiles from the Mapbox servers.
\end{itemize}

\section{Software Requirements}

The development and execution of the system rely on a robust stack of modern software frameworks and cloud services:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Operating System:} Android (Version 9.0 Pie or newer) or iOS (Version 12.0 or newer).
\item \textbf{Frontend Framework:} Flutter SDK (Dart programming language) for building a single, cross-platform codebase that compiles to native ARM code for both platforms.
\item \textbf{Mapping Service:} Mapbox SDK. This is utilized over standard Google Maps due to its highly customizable styling, optimized tile rendering, and support for complex geospatial datasets (Safe Zone suggestions and high-density map clustering).
\item \textbf{Database \& Backend Integration:} Supabase. The system uses PostgreSQL as the primary relational database and heavily utilizes Supabase WebSockets to push millisecond-latency updates to connected clients.
\item \textbf{Algorithmic Frameworks:} Custom implementations of weighted scoring logic and Greedy Clustering. These algorithms are designed to operate in linear $O(N)$ time complexity to ensure they do not block the UI thread on older mobile devices.
\end{itemize}

\section{Functional Requirements}

The system must perform the following core functions to be considered successful:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Allow users to create "Lost" or "Found" posts containing geospatial coordinates, timestamps, and categorical tags.
    \item Automatically calculate a match probability between lost and found items in the database.
    \item Obfuscate the images of "Found" items to public users to prevent fraudulent claims.
    \item Generate a unique, time-sensitive QR code for the finder, which the claimant must scan to verify the handover.
    \item Render item locations on an interactive map, clustering pins together as the user zooms out to prevent UI clutter.
\end{itemize}

\section{Non-Functional Requirements}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Real-Time Latency:} Updates regarding item status or chat messages must reflect on the client device in under 500 milliseconds.
    \item \textbf{Scalability:} The clustering algorithm must be able to process and render up to 10,000 active pins on the map without dropping the application frame rate below 30 FPS.
    \item \textbf{Security:} All user data and chat logs must be secured via Row Level Security (RLS) policies in PostgreSQL.
\end{itemize}

%---------------- CHAPTER 4 ----------------%
\chapter{System Development}

\section{Existing System}

The existing system for managing lost and found operations in modern society is highly fragmented. Currently, discovery relies almost entirely on manual effort. Users must perform manual keyword searches on localized platforms or continuously scroll through disorganized social media groups (e.g., Facebook community pages, college WhatsApp groups). The organization of this data is exceptionally poor, typically presented as an endless, unsorted vertical feed with absolutely no spatial context regarding where the item was actually lost.

Furthermore, the existing system suffers from severe privacy and security vulnerabilities. When a well-meaning individual finds an item, they traditionally post a clear, high-resolution photo of the object to a public forum. This creates a massive security flaw known as the "Claim Scam." Malicious actors view the public photo, note the specific details (scratches, brand, color), and use those exact details to falsely claim ownership to the finder. Finally, the physical handover of items is entirely unverified. Meeting a stranger from the internet to hand over a valuable item exposes both the finder and the loser to potential physical risk, theft, or extortion.

\section{Proposed System}

To rectify the severe flaws of traditional methods, we propose "Reunite", a mobile application that introduces advanced cryptographic security, automated backend matching, and dynamic mapping capabilities. The proposed system shifts the paradigm from manual searching to algorithmic pairing.

The system is defined by four core technological pillars:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Asymmetric Privacy Protocol:} "Found" posts deliberately hide crucial identifying details. For example, high-resolution images are heavily blurred or obscured using backend image processing. To claim an item, a potential owner must mathematically or cryptographically prove prior knowledge of the item's hidden attributes.
    \item \textbf{Automated Scoring Engine:} A custom backend algorithm that actively scans the database. It calculates match probabilities based on Geolocation Proximity ($S_{geo}$), Chronological Freshness ($V_{time}$), and Tag/Attribute Similarity ($S_{attr}$). 
    \item \textbf{Geospatial Clustering:} To solve the "Map Clutter" issue, the app utilizes an $O(N)$ greedy clustering algorithm. As the user zooms out (e.g., from a street-level view to a city-wide view), individual pins dynamically merge into clustered nodes displaying the total count of items in that sector.
    \item \textbf{Cryptographic QR Verification:} A secure handover protocol that removes human error. When the parties meet, the finder's app generates a cryptographically secure, time-sensitive QR code. The owner must scan this code with their app to officially mark the item as "Returned," leaving an immutable audit trail in the database.
\end{itemize}

\section{Advantages of the Proposed System}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Scam Immunity:} The Asymmetric Privacy protocol completely eliminates visual bait. Because scammers cannot see the specific details of a found item, they cannot formulate convincing fake ownership claims.
\item \textbf{UX Clarity \& Spatial Awareness:} Spatial clustering keeps maps usable and readable, even in high-loss urban zones like universities or concert venues. Users gain immediate spatial context rather than reading vague text descriptions.
\item \textbf{High Performance:} By designing the clustering and filtering algorithms to operate in $O(N)$ linear time complexity, the application ensures smooth 60 FPS rendering and functionality, even on older, budget-tier mobile devices.
\item \textbf{Real-Time Agility:} Supabase WebSockets push data to connected clients instantly. This completely eliminates "Ghost Data" issues; when an item is claimed, it vanishes from all users' maps within milliseconds.
\end{itemize}

\section{Detailed Module Description}

To ensure high maintainability and separation of concerns, the Reunite system is divided into distinct operational modules.

\subsection{Matching and Scoring Engine}
This module acts as the backend "brain" of the application. It automatically calculates the probability that a newly "Found" item matches an existing "Lost" item. The algorithm weighs three primary factors: Proximity (how close the lost and found coordinates are), Freshness (the time delta between the two reports), and Attribute Similarity (matching categorical tags). If the combined $mScore$ exceeds a predefined threshold, the system automatically alerts both users of a "Match Zone."

\subsection{Asymmetric Privacy Controller}
This module governs the "Trust Network." Its primary function is to prevent fraudulent claims by actively obfuscating item details. When a user uploads a "Found" item, this controller intercepts the image, generates a heavily blurred thumbnail for public display, and locks the high-resolution image in a secure bucket. The controller only releases the clear image and specific details once a claimant's attributes match the hidden attributes with high confidence.

\subsection{Map Visualization \& Clustering Module}
This module interfaces directly with the Mapbox SDK. It is responsible for translating raw latitude and longitude coordinates from the database into interactive visual markers. To prevent the UI from becoming overwhelmed in dense areas, the module executes a Greedy Clustering algorithm that groups nearby pins based on the current camera zoom level. It also calculates and visualizes geographic "Safe Zones" (e.g., well-lit public areas or campus security offices) for users to conduct handovers.

\subsection{Real-Time Data Sync Module}
Built entirely upon Supabase WebSockets, this module maintains a persistent connection between the mobile client and the PostgreSQL database. It listens for `INSERT`, `UPDATE`, and `DELETE` payloads. When an item is marked as recovered, this module instantly broadcasts a delete event to all active clients, automatically scrubbing the pin from their maps and eliminating ghost data.

%---------------- CHAPTER 5 ----------------%
\chapter{System Design}

\section{System Architecture}

The system architecture of the Reunite platform is designed as a modern, decoupled client-server model optimized to handle high-frequency geospatial data and real-time algorithmic matching. The architecture is divided into the Client Layer and the Backend/Data Layer.

\subsection{1. The Client Layer (Flutter Mobile App)}
The client layer is the user-facing portion of the system, responsible for capturing input and rendering complex visual states.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Cross-Platform UI:} The application is built using Flutter, allowing a single Dart codebase to compile natively for both Android and iOS devices, ensuring visual consistency.
    \item \textbf{Spatial Rendering (Mapbox):} Integrates the Mapbox SDK to handle the heavy lifting of rendering vector map tiles, plotting geographic coordinates, and dynamically drawing "Safe Zone" polygons.
    \item \textbf{Client-Side Clustering:} To reduce server strain, the $O(N)$ greedy clustering algorithm is executed directly on the client's processor. It groups coordinates based on the viewport bounds and zoom level before rendering the markers to the screen.
    \item \textbf{Hardware Integration:} The client directly accesses native device APIs to utilize the camera (for capturing item photos and scanning QR codes) and the GPS module (for capturing precise lat/long coordinates upon posting).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{2. The Backend Layer (Supabase / PostgreSQL)}
The backend layer handles data persistence, security protocols, and heavy algorithmic processing.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Real-Time Data Sync:} Utilizes Supabase WebSockets to deliver millisecond data updates across the network. This bypasses the need for the client to constantly poll the server via HTTP REST requests.
    \item \textbf{Asymmetric Privacy Controller:} Deployed as middleware, this controller manages the "Trust Network." It dictates Row Level Security (RLS) policies in PostgreSQL, ensuring that unauthorized users cannot query the database for hidden attributes of found items.
    \item \textbf{Matching Engine (The "Brain"):} Relies on Supabase Edge Functions to execute the custom scoring logic on the cloud. By utilizing the PostGIS extension within PostgreSQL, the database can rapidly perform complex spatial queries (e.g., finding all items within a 5km radius) to feed into the scoring algorithm.
\end{itemize}

\section{Database Schema and Security}
The underlying database relies heavily on the PostGIS extension. Instead of storing location as simple text strings, coordinates are stored as `GEOGRAPHY(Point)` data types. This allows the backend to mathematically index locations, drastically speeding up distance calculations required by the Matching Engine. Furthermore, all image data is stored in Supabase Storage buckets, with the URLs securely linked to the relational tables. Access to these buckets is tightly controlled by the Asymmetric Privacy policies.

\subsection{Matching and Scoring Logic ($mScore$)}
The core algorithm for determining if a "Lost" item matches a "Found" item involves calculating a weighted mathematical score based on geographic distance and categorical attributes.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Dart]
// Calculate spatial proximity score (0.0 to 1.0)
double sGeo = (1 - (distance / 0.05)).clamp(0.0, 1.0);

// Check strict category alignment
bool categoryMatch = targetLost['category'] != null && 
                     targetLost['category'] == found['category'];

String lostTags = (targetLost['tags'] ?? "").toString().toLowerCase();
String foundTags = (found['tags'] ?? "").toString().toLowerCase();

// Calculate attribute similarity score
double sAttr = (lostTags.isNotEmpty && 
                foundTags.contains(lostTags.split(',').first.trim())) 
                ? 1.0 : 0.0;

// Final Weighted Score Calculation
double mScore = (sGeo * 0.4) + (categoryMatch ? 0.4 : 0.0) + (sAttr * 0.2);

// Heavily penalize the score if the root categories do not match
if (!categoryMatch) mScore *= 0.05;
\end{lstlisting}
\textit{Explanation:} The $sGeo$ variable normalizes the physical distance between items. The algorithm gives a 40\% weight to geographic proximity, a 40\% weight to a strict category match (e.g., Electronics to Electronics), and a 20\% weight to specific tags (e.g., "Apple", "Blue"). If the broad categories do not match at all, the total score is decimated to prevent false positives.

\chapter{Conclusion and Future Scope}

\section{Conclusion}

This initiative has successfully culminated in the development of "Reunite," a robust, cross-platform mobile application that fundamentally redefines how communities handle lost and found items. By shifting away from chaotic social media feeds and integrating modern real-time databases with advanced geospatial mapping, we have addressed the critical security and efficiency flaws present in traditional recovery management.

\textbf{Paradigm Shift:} Reunite successfully transforms the lost-and-found process from a passive "waiting game" into an active recovery mission. Instead of relying on manual scrolling and keyword guessing, the system acts autonomously, utilizing a mathematical scoring engine to link users based on empirical spatial and categorical data points.

\textbf{Security Enhancement:} The application completely replaces vague, vulnerable public posts with an algorithmically secured system. By implementing the Asymmetric Privacy protocol, we have effectively neutralized the "Claim Scam," ensuring that sensitive visual details are obfuscated and that only the rightful owner can verify the details of a found item. Furthermore, the QR Verification protocol removes human error and physical risk from the final handover process.

\textbf{Final Vision:} Ultimately, we have created a system that is not just a digital map, but a functioning "Trust Network" for the community. Reunite ensures safe physical handovers, eliminates map clutter through intelligent clustering, and provides immediate, real-time spatial clarity to users in distress.

\section{Future Scope}

Although the Reunite system provides a powerful and secure foundation for managing lost items, several highly impactful avenues for future technological enhancement exist:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{AI Image Recognition Integration:} Future iterations will integrate Cloud Vision APIs (such as Google Cloud Vision or OpenCV) to automatically scan user-uploaded photos. The AI will automatically extract tags, colors, and brands, eliminating user input error and drastically improving the accuracy of the $S_{attr}$ (Similarity Score) calculation.
    \item \textbf{Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) Integration:} The app can be expanded to utilize BLE scanning to detect nearby smart-tags (such as Apple AirTags, Tile, or Samsung SmartTags). If a user reports a lost item with a BLE beacon, other users running the Reunite app in the background can act as a passive mesh network, anonymously updating the item's location if they walk past it.
    \item \textbf{Institutional Web Dashboards:} Creating a specialized, web-based admin dashboard tailored for large institutions (university campuses, airports, shopping malls). This would allow campus security or lost-and-found departments to manage large-scale inventories through our secure backend, digitizing the traditional "cardboard box of lost items" while directly connecting to the student mobile app network.
    \item \textbf{Advanced Predictive Movement:} Implementing machine learning algorithms to analyze historical lost-and-found data. The system could predict the trajectory of items left on public transit, automatically shifting the "Match Zone" polygons along train lines or bus routes based on transit timetables.
\end{itemize}
